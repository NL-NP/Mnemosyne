Definitions:
1. A Point is that which has no parts.
Euclid probably meant that a point is indivisible, and thus cannot be broken into parts. It’s a vague idea, Hilbert defined points as fundamental entities, and that is how I am going to describe it too, for having something more fundamental than a point — in Euclidean geometry specifically, might hinder the point of the essence Euclidean Geometry is trying to convey, which is based on the structure of points, lines, and surfaces.

For a single point, there shall exist no dimension, in which we define dimension as an independent axis of relating measurements into magnitudes, in which one-dimensional means relating only 1 magnitude, often worded as the length in Euclid’s elements. Two dimensional, relating two magnitudes, relating length in breadth. Three-dimensional, relating length, breadth, and depth. In modern wording, we often refer to them as length, width, and height. It’s worth keeping in mind that Euclid’ probably meant that magnitude is how thick or thin something is, and not specifically applying the idea of dimension here.

These will be the only dimensions that we will be working on. As a side note, in this series, when I say depth, you should imply that there already exist breadth and length, when I say breadth, you should imply there exists length, this is more of an order thing with Euclid’s Elements, most likely this kind of order won’t be used outside of the portions of Euclid’s elements, nor was the order used in modern schools anyway. 


2. A line is breadthless length.

We’re not here to ask if a line feels like it should be one-dimensional. We’re here to say, if we define a line this way, what follows?”

In summary, for a line to be of a singular dimension the ways you relate each point that lies on the line shall be about another point on the line, and there shouldn’t be any exceptions, and since within this line the only difference is the difference in magnitude under the same, well, “frame of reference”, it is one dimensional. Common Notions
Before going onto the common notions, I would like to note a few facts about the essence of equality in geometry.
When in the context of geometry, we assume that we are comparing magnitudes, not the properties of geometric figures. By properties, I meant to differ in rotation, orientation, and so on.
Certainly, a lot of times it may seem to be comparing the properties of geometric figures, but they should all be measurable quantities, for example, lengths and the size of angles.

For example, when comparing the equality of two straight lines, when line A =  line B, we shall assume the lengths are the same.

For context, the difference between Common notions and postulates is that Common notions are the common truth, or axioms, the things we assume to be true in logical reasoning, while postulates are axioms in Euclid's elements.

Common notions1. Things which are equal to the same thing are also equal to one another. 
If A = B and B = C, then A = C, also known as the transitive property, we won’t be exploring that in this series.
Common notion 2.  If equal be added to equals, the wholes are equal.
If A = B, then A plus c, a constant, is equal to B plus c.

3. If equal is subtracted from equal, the remainder is equal
If A = B, then A minus c, a constant, is equal to B minus c.

4. Things which coincide with one another are equal to one another
If one thing can completely overlap another exactly, same size, same shape. They are equal to each other

5. The whole is greater than the parts

Self-explanatory, From a whole we take parts of it out, for example, Let a straight line be bounded by points A and B, we insert a new point C internal of the definite straight line AB, the 2 new straight lines that could be described shall be AC and CB, AC on its own shall always be less than AB, so is BC, which shall also always be less than line AB. I would like to note again that common notions are also axioms, so if you wish to create a framework saying that common notions 4 or 5 are not axioms, that’s fine, but we are sticking to these axioms.
